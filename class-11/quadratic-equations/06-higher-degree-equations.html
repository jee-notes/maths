Higher Degree Polynomial Equations

Extending quadratics: Vieta's generalized formulas, polynomial structures, and root transformations

General Theory of Equations

A second-degree quadratic equation is merely the starting point of polynomial algebra. The same structural rules governing quadratics extend naturally to polynomial equations of higher degrees.

A general **polynomial equation of degree \(n\)** is represented as: \[a_n x^n + a_{n-1} x^{n-1} + a_{n-2} x^{n-2} + \dots + a_1 x + a_0 = 0 \quad (a_n \neq 0)\] where \(a_n, a_{n-1}, \dots, a_0\) are real or complex coefficients.

By the **Fundamental Theorem of Algebra**, a polynomial equation of degree \(n\) has **exactly \(n\) roots** (which may be real, complex, distinct, or repeating) in the complex field.

Vieta's generalized Relations

If the roots of the degree-\(n\) polynomial are \(\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \dots, \alpha_n\), the polynomial can be factored as: \[a_n x^n + a_{n-1} x^{n-1} + \dots + a_0 = a_n (x - \alpha_1)(x - \alpha_2)\dots(x - \alpha_n)\] By expanding the right-hand side and equating corresponding coefficients, we obtain **Vieta's Generalized Formulas** relating the roots and coefficients:

1. Cubic Equations (Degree 3)

For a cubic equation with roots \(\alpha, \beta, \gamma\): \[ax^3 + bx^2 + cx + d = 0 \quad (a \neq 0)\] The root-coefficient relations are:
  1. Sum of roots taken one at a time (\(S_1\)): \[\alpha + \beta + \gamma = -\frac{b}{a}\]
  2. Sum of products taken two at a time (\(S_2\)): \[\alpha\beta + \beta\gamma + \gamma\alpha = \frac{c}{a}\]
  3. Product of roots (\(S_3\)): \[\alpha\beta\gamma = -\frac{d}{a}\]

2. Biquadratic / Quartic Equations (Degree 4)

For a biquadratic equation with roots \(\alpha, \beta, \gamma, \delta\): \[ax^4 + bx^3 + cx^2 + dx + e = 0 \quad (a \neq 0)\] The root-coefficient relations are:
  1. Sum of roots taken one at a time (\(S_1\)): \[\alpha + \beta + \gamma + \delta = -\frac{b}{a}\]
  2. Sum of products taken two at a time (\(S_2\)): \[\alpha\beta + \alpha\gamma + \alpha\delta + \beta\gamma + \beta\delta + \gamma\delta = \frac{c}{a}\]
  3. Sum of products taken three at a time (\(S_3\)): \[\alpha\beta\gamma + \alpha\beta\delta + \alpha\gamma\delta + \beta\gamma\delta = -\frac{d}{a}\]
  4. Product of roots taken four at a time (\(S_4\)): \[\alpha\beta\gamma\delta = \frac{e}{a}\]

The Alternating Sign Rule

Notice the beautiful symmetry in Vieta's formulas: the denominators are always the leading coefficient (\(a_n\)), and the signs of the fractions strictly alternate, beginning with a negative sign: \[S_k = (-1)^k \frac{a_{n-k}}{a_n}\] This rule holds true for polynomials of any arbitrary degree!

Transformation of Equations

Transformation of equations is a powerful algebraic shortcut in JEE. If you are given a polynomial equation \(f(x) = 0\) with roots \(\alpha, \beta, \gamma, \dots\), you can formulate a new equation whose roots are symmetric modifications of these roots without ever calculating the individual roots.

Desired Roots of New Equation Required Substitution in \(f(x) = 0\) Form of New Equation
Reciprocal roots: \(\frac{1}{\alpha}, \frac{1}{\beta}, \dots\) Substitute \(x = \frac{1}{y}\) \(f\left(\frac{1}{y}\right) = 0\)
Negative roots: \(-\alpha, -\beta, \dots\) Substitute \(x = -y\) \(f(-y) = 0\)
Multiplied by a constant: \(k\alpha, k\beta, \dots\) Substitute \(x = \frac{y}{k}\) \(f\left(\frac{y}{k}\right) = 0\)
Shifted by a constant: \(\alpha + h, \beta + h, \dots\) Substitute \(x = y - h\) \(f(y - h) = 0\)
Squared roots: \(\alpha^2, \beta^2, \dots\) Substitute \(x = \sqrt{y}\) \(f(\sqrt{y}) = 0 \quad\) (Rationalize to remove radical)

Solved Examples

Example 1: Evaluating Symmetric Functions in Cubic Equations

Problem: Let \(\alpha, \beta, \gamma\) be the roots of the cubic equation: \[x^3 - px^2 + qx - r = 0\] Find the algebraic values of the following expressions in terms of \(p, q, r\):
  1. \(\alpha^2 + \beta^2 + \gamma^2\)
  2. \(\frac{1}{\alpha\beta} + \frac{1}{\beta\gamma} + \frac{1}{\gamma\alpha}\)
Solution: First, let's write down Vieta's formulas for our cubic equation (where \(a = 1, b = -p, c = q, d = -r\)): \[\alpha + \beta + \gamma = -\frac{-p}{1} = p\] \[\alpha\beta + \beta\gamma + \gamma\alpha = \frac{q}{1} = q\] \[\alpha\beta\gamma = -\frac{-r}{1} = r\] Now we evaluate each part:
  1. Evaluate \(\alpha^2 + \beta^2 + \gamma^2\): Using the algebraic expansion identity for three variables: \[(\alpha + \beta + \gamma)^2 = \alpha^2 + \beta^2 + \gamma^2 + 2(\alpha\beta + \beta\gamma + \gamma\alpha)\] Rearrange terms to isolate the sum of squares: \[\alpha^2 + \beta^2 + \gamma^2 = (\alpha + \beta + \gamma)^2 - 2(\alpha\beta + \beta\gamma + \gamma\alpha)\] Substitute our Vieta relations:
    \[\alpha^2 + \beta^2 + \gamma^2 = p^2 - 2q\]
  2. Evaluate \(\frac{1}{\alpha\beta} + \frac{1}{\beta\gamma} + \frac{1}{\gamma\alpha}\): Combine the three fractions by finding a common denominator, which is \(\alpha\beta\gamma\): \[\frac{1}{\alpha\beta} + \frac{1}{\beta\gamma} + \frac{1}{\gamma\alpha} = \frac{\gamma + \alpha + \beta}{\alpha\beta\gamma}\] Rearranging the numerator: \[\frac{1}{\alpha\beta} + \frac{1}{\beta\gamma} + \frac{1}{\gamma\alpha} = \frac{\alpha + \beta + \gamma}{\alpha\beta\gamma}\] Substitute our Vieta relations:
    \[\frac{1}{\alpha\beta} + \frac{1}{\beta\gamma} + \frac{1}{\gamma\alpha} = \frac{p}{r}\]

Example 2: Formulating Reciprocal Cubic Equations

Problem: Form a polynomial equation whose roots are the reciprocals of the roots of the cubic equation: \[3x^3 - 5x^2 + 2x - 7 = 0\]
Solution: Let \(\alpha, \beta, \gamma\) be the roots of the original cubic equation \(3x^3 - 5x^2 + 2x - 7 = 0\). We are looking for an equation with roots: \[\alpha' = \frac{1}{\alpha}, \quad \beta' = \frac{1}{\beta}, \quad \gamma' = \frac{1}{\gamma}\] Using the **Transformation of Equations** tool, we make the substitution \(x = \frac{1}{y}\) into the original equation: \[3\left(\frac{1}{y}\right)^3 - 5\left(\frac{1}{y}\right)^2 + 2\left(\frac{1}{y}\right) - 7 = 0\] \[3\left(\frac{1}{y^3}\right) - 5\left(\frac{1}{y^2}\right) + 2\left(\frac{1}{y}\right) - 7 = 0\] Multiply the entire equation by \(y^3\) to eliminate all denominators: \[3 - 5y + 2y^2 - 7y^3 = 0\] Multiply by \(-1\) to write the polynomial in standard descending order:
\[7y^3 - 2y^2 + 5y - 3 = 0\]
Replacing the dummy variable \(y\) with our standard variable \(x\): \[7x^3 - 2x^2 + 5x - 3 = 0\] Conclusion: The reciprocal cubic equation is \(7x^3 - 2x^2 + 5x - 3 = 0\).

Insight Note: Notice the beautiful pattern! For a reciprocal transformation, the coefficients of the polynomial are simply written in the **exact reverse order**: \[\text{Original: } [3, -5, 2, -7] \quad \longrightarrow \quad \text{Reciprocal: } [-7, 2, -5, 3] \equiv [7, -2, 5, -3]\] This reverse-coefficient shortcut works for polynomials of any degree, saving valuable seconds in high-speed exams!
