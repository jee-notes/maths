Location of Roots

Parametric positioning: mapping roots relative to real numbers and interval boundaries

Introduction to Location of Roots

In standard algebra, we solve for roots under fixed coefficients. However, JEE problems frequently ask us to solve **parametric equations** where the coefficients are unknown parameters, and we must find the range of these parameters such that the roots lie within specific real-number intervals.

This topic requires combining three separate mathematical layers:

  1. **The existence of roots** (Discriminant \(D\)).
  2. **The orientation of the curve** (Value of the function at boundary points \(f(d)\)).
  3. **The physical position of the curve** (The horizontal position of the vertex \(-\frac{b}{2a}\)).

Pro-Tip: To make our constraints universal, we always frame them using the product \(a \cdot f(k)\) rather than just \(f(k)\). This accounts for both upward-opening (\(a > 0\)) and downward-opening (\(a < 0\)) parabolas.

The 5 Classical Cases

Let \(f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c\) be a quadratic expression with real roots \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\). Let \(d\) and \(e\) be real numbers (with \(d < e\)).

Case I: Both roots are greater than a real number \(d\) (\(\alpha, \beta > d\))

Geometrically, the entire active segment of the parabola must lie to the right of the vertical line \(x = d\).

Simultaneous Constraints:
  1. Roots must exist: \(D \ge 0\) (Allows for equal or distinct roots).
  2. Vertex must lie to the right of \(d\): \(-\frac{b}{2a} > d\).
  3. Sign at the boundary point: \(a \cdot f(d) > 0\).

Case II: Both roots are less than a real number \(d\) (\(\alpha, \beta < d\))

Geometrically, the active segment of the parabola lies to the left of the vertical line \(x = d\).

Simultaneous Constraints:
  1. Roots must exist: \(D \ge 0\).
  2. Vertex must lie to the left of \(d\): \(-\frac{b}{2a} < d\).
  3. Sign at the boundary point: \(a \cdot f(d) > 0\).

Case III: A real number \(d\) lies between the roots (\(\alpha < d < \beta\))

Geometrically, the point \((d, f(d))\) must lie inside the "cup" of the parabola.

Constraint:
\[a \cdot f(d) < 0\]
Insight Note: This single constraint is incredibly elegant. If \(a \cdot f(d) < 0\), the parabola is forced to cross the x-axis on both sides of \(d\) to return to its asymptotic direction. Thus, **real and distinct roots are guaranteed automatically**, making additional constraints like \(D > 0\) or vertex checks completely redundant!

Case IV: Both roots lie within an open interval \((d, e)\) (\(d < \alpha, \beta < e\))

Geometrically, the roots must be trapped between the vertical barriers \(x = d\) and \(x = e\).

Simultaneous Constraints:
  1. Roots must exist: \(D \ge 0\).
  2. Vertex must lie inside the interval: \(d < -\frac{b}{2a} < e\).
  3. Sign at the left barrier: \(a \cdot f(d) > 0\).
  4. Sign at the right barrier: \(a \cdot f(e) > 0\).

Case V: Exactly one root lies within the open interval \((d, e)\)

Geometrically, the curve must cross the x-axis exactly once between \(d\) and \(e\), meaning the function changes sign between the two barriers.

Constraint:
\[f(d) \cdot f(e) < 0\]
Warning on Boundary Cases: If a root can coincide exactly with a boundary point (e.g. \(\alpha = d\) or \(\beta = e\)), then \(f(d) \cdot f(e) = 0\). You must test the conditions \(f(d) = 0\) and \(f(e) = 0\) manually to see if they satisfy the problem statement.

Solved Examples

Example 1: Parameter Domain for Roots Greater than a Bound

Problem: Find all real values of the parameter \(a\) for which both roots of the quadratic equation: \[x^2 - 6ax + (2 - 2a + 9a^2) = 0\] are greater than \(3\).
Solution: Let \(f(x) = x^2 - 6ax + (2 - 2a + 9a^2)\). This is a quadratic equation with leading coefficient \(1 > 0\).

We apply **Case I** constraints with \(d = 3\):
  1. Constraint 1: \(D \ge 0\) \[D = (-6a)^2 - 4(1)(2 - 2a + 9a^2) \ge 0\] \[36a^2 - 8 + 8a - 36a^2 \ge 0\] Notice that the quadratic terms cancel out: \[8a - 8 \ge 0 \implies a \ge 1\]
  2. Constraint 2: \(-\frac{b}{2a} > 3\) Here, \(b = -6a\) and \(a_{\text{coeff}} = 1\): \[-\frac{-6a}{2(1)} > 3 \implies 3a > 3 \implies a > 1\]
  3. Constraint 3: \(f(3) > 0\) \[f(3) = (3)^2 - 6a(3) + (2 - 2a + 9a^2) > 0\] \[9 - 18a + 2 - 2a + 9a^2 > 0\] \[9a^2 - 20a + 11 > 0\] Factor the quadratic expression: \[(9a - 11)(a - 1) > 0\] The solution to this inequality is: \[a \in (-\infty, 1) \cup \left( \frac{11}{9}, \infty \right)\]
Now, we must find the intersection of all three constraint sets:
  • From Constraint 1: \(a \ge 1\)
  • From Constraint 2: \(a > 1\)
  • From Constraint 3: \(a \in (-\infty, 1) \cup \left( \frac{11}{9}, \infty \right)\)
Intersecting \(a > 1\) with the third set gives: \[a \in \left( \frac{11}{9}, \infty \right)\] Conclusion: Both roots are greater than \(3\) if and only if \(a \in \left( \frac{11}{9}, \infty \right)\).

Example 2: Complex Interval Constraints (Roots Bracketing Points)

Problem: Find all real values of \(m\) for which the quadratic equation: \[x^2 - (m - 3)x + m = 0\] has one root smaller than \(1\) and the other root greater than \(2\).
Solution: Let's analyze what this statement means geometrically. If one root is smaller than \(1\) (\(\alpha < 1\)) and the other root is greater than \(2\) (\(\beta > 2\)), then both real numbers \(1\) and \(2\) must lie strictly **between** the two roots: \[\alpha < 1 < 2 < \beta\] This is a double-boundary variation of **Case III**.

For both \(1\) and \(2\) to lie inside the roots of an upward-opening parabola (\(a = 1 > 0\)), we must satisfy two simple conditions simultaneously:
  1. The value at \(x = 1\) must be negative: \(f(1) < 0\)
  2. The value at \(x = 2\) must be negative: \(f(2) < 0\)
Let's define the function: \[f(x) = x^2 - (m - 3)x + m\]
  1. Apply constraint \(f(1) < 0\): \[f(1) = 1^2 - (m - 3)(1) + m < 0\] \[f(1) = 1 - m + 3 + m < 0\] Observe the cancellation of \(m\): \[4 < 0\] This is a logical contradiction! The inequality \(4 < 0\) is never true for any real number \(m\).
Since \(f(1) = 4\) is constant and always positive, the parabola can never dip below the x-axis at \(x = 1\). Thus, it is mathematically impossible for the root \(\alpha\) to be less than \(1\) while maintaining real coefficients.

Conclusion: There are **no real values of \(m\)** for which the roots satisfy the given conditions.
