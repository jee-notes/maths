Logarithmic Equations

Systematic methods for solving algebraic equations containing logarithmic operators

What is a Logarithmic Equation?

A logarithmic equation is an equation in which a variable appears inside the argument or base of a logarithmic term. Examples include:

\[\log_2 (x - 1) + \log_2 (x + 2) = 2\] \[\log_x (3x^2 - x) = 2\]

To solve these equations, we employ algebraic identities to simplify and remove the logarithmic operators. However, removing logarithms algebraically comes with a massive trap.

The Golden Rule: Domain Check & Extraneous Roots

When solving logarithmic equations, the most critical step is the **final domain check**.

Algebraic manipulation (like the product or quotient rule) often yields a simplified algebraic equation that has a larger domain than the original logarithmic equation. The roots of this simplified equation that do not satisfy the original constraints are called **extraneous roots**.

The Domain Check Protocol

For every candidate root \(x_0\) obtained after algebraic simplification, you must substitute \(x_0\) back into the original form of the equation and check:

  1. Are all logarithmic arguments strictly positive? (\(> 0\))
  2. Are all logarithmic bases positive and not equal to 1? (\(> 0\) and \(\neq 1\))

If a candidate root fails any constraint in even a single term of the original equation, it must be **rejected**.

Insight: How Algebraic Rules Expand the Domain

Consider the identity \(\log_a(x) + \log_a(y) = \log_a(xy)\).

  • On the left-hand side, the expression is defined only if \(x > 0\) and \(y > 0\).
  • On the right-hand side, \(\log_a(xy)\) is defined if \(xy > 0\). This product is positive if both \(x\) and \(y\) are positive, **or** if both \(x\) and \(y\) are negative!

If we algebraically transition from left to right, we inadvertently allow negative values of \(x\) and \(y\) into our equations. The resulting algebraic solutions can yield negative values which satisfy the simplified equation but violate the original terms.

Standard Solving Techniques

Most JEE logarithmic equations fall into one of three structural categories:

1. Equations Reducible to Quadratic Forms

If the equation consists of terms containing \(\log_a x\) and its powers, we substitute \(t = \log_a x\). This transforms the expression into a standard polynomial or rational equation in \(t\). Once \(t\) is solved, we find \(x = a^t\).

2. Equations with Variables in Exponents

When an equation contains terms of the form \(f(x)^{g(x)} = c\), we "take logarithms on both sides" to pull the exponents down using the power rule.

3. Base-Change Reductions

If the logarithms in the equation have different bases (e.g., base 2, 4, and 16), use the base-change theorem or the base-power identity to convert all terms to a single common base.

Solved Examples

Example 1: Base-Change Reduction

Solve for \(x \in \mathbb{R}\): \[\log_2 x + \log_4 x + \log_{16} x = 7\]

Solution:

Step 1: Domain check constraint. The argument is \(x\), so we must have \(x > 0\).

Step 2: Base harmonization. Express all bases as powers of 2:

  • \(\log_2 x\)
  • \(\log_4 x = \log_{2^2} x = \frac{1}{2} \log_2 x\)
  • \(\log_{16} x = \log_{2^4} x = \frac{1}{4} \log_2 x\)
Substitute these into the equation: \[\log_2 x + \frac{1}{2} \log_2 x + \frac{1}{4} \log_2 x = 7\]

Factoring out \(\log_2 x\): \[\left(1 + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4}\right) \log_2 x = 7\] \[\left(\frac{4 + 2 + 1}{4}\right) \log_2 x = 7\] \[\frac{7}{4} \log_2 x = 7\] \[\log_2 x = 4\]

By definition: \[x = 2^4 = 16\] Since \(16 > 0\), the constraint is satisfied.

Answer: \(x = 16\)

Example 2: Extraneous Root Detection with Variable Base

Solve for \(x \in \mathbb{R}\): \[x^{\log_x (x+3)} = 5\]

Solution:

Step 1: Identify constraints from the original expression.

  • Base of the logarithm: \(x > 0\) and \(x \neq 1\).
  • Argument of the logarithm: \(x + 3 > 0 \implies x > -3\).
Intersection of these constraints yields the overall domain: **\(x \in (0, 1) \cup (1, \infty)\)**.

Step 2: Solve the equation. Using the fundamental identity \(b^{\log_b N} = N\) (where \(b = x > 0, b \neq 1\) and \(N = x+3 > 0\)): \[x^{\log_x (x+3)} = x + 3\] The equation reduces to: \[x + 3 = 5\] \[x = 2\]

Step 3: Verification. Check if \(x = 2\) lies in our domain: \(2 > 0\) and \(2 \neq 1\). The constraints are fully satisfied.

Answer: \(x = 2\)

Example 3: Variables in Base and Arguments on Both Sides

Solve for \(x \in \mathbb{R}\): \[\log_{(x+3)} (x^2 - x) = \log_{(x+3)} (2x + 4)\]

Solution:

Step 1: Domain Constraints.

  1. Base: \(x+3 > 0\) and \(x+3 \neq 1 \implies x > -3\) and \(x \neq -2\).
  2. First argument: \(x^2 - x > 0 \implies x(x-1) > 0 \implies x \in (-\infty, 0) \cup (1, \infty)\).
  3. Second argument: \(2x + 4 > 0 \implies 2x > -4 \implies x > -2\).
Taking the intersection of all three conditions:
  • \(x > -3\) and \(x \neq -2\)
  • \(x > -2\) (this already satisfies \(x > -3\) and \(x \neq -2\))
  • \(x < 0\) or \(x > 1\)
Thus, the valid domain is: **\(x \in (-2, 0) \cup (1, \infty)\)**.

Step 2: Solve the equation. Equating the arguments: \[x^2 - x = 2x + 4\] \[x^2 - 3x - 4 = 0\] Factoring the quadratic: \[(x - 4)(x + 1) = 0 \implies x = 4 \text{ or } x = -1\]

Step 3: Verify roots against the domain.

  • For \(x = 4\): Lies in \((1, \infty)\). **Valid.**
  • For \(x = -1\): Lies in \((-2, 0)\). **Valid.**
Both roots satisfy all original base and argument constraints.

Answer: \(x = -1, 4\)
