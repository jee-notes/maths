Harmonic Progression & Means

Reciprocal linearity, bounding inequalities, and the hierarchy of means

Harmonic Progression (H.P.)

Unlike Arithmetic and Geometric Progressions, a Harmonic Progression does not possess an independent defining formula for its terms. Instead, it is defined entirely in relation to an Arithmetic Progression.

Definition of an H.P.

A sequence of non-zero numbers \(h_1, h_2, h_3, \dots, h_n\) is a Harmonic Progression (H.P.) if and only if their reciprocals form an Arithmetic Progression (A.P.): \[\frac{1}{h_1}, \ \frac{1}{h_2}, \ \frac{1}{h_3}, \ \dots, \ \frac{1}{h_n} \quad \text{are in A.P.}\]

Because an H.P. is essentially a "reciprocal A.P.", there is no general formula for the sum of \(n\) terms of an H.P. To find any \(n^{\text{th}}\) term of an H.P., you must:

  1. Invert the terms to form an A.P.
  2. Calculate the \(n^{\text{th}}\) term of the resulting A.P. using \(T_n = a + (n-1)d\).
  3. Reciprocate this result to find the \(n^{\text{th}}\) term of the original H.P.

Harmonic Mean (H.M.)

If three numbers \(a, b, c\) are in H.P., the middle term \(b\) is the **Harmonic Mean (H.M.)** between \(a\) and \(c\).

By definition, their reciprocals \(\frac{1}{a}, \frac{1}{b}, \frac{1}{c}\) must be in A.P. Therefore: \[\frac{1}{b} - \frac{1}{a} = \frac{1}{c} - \frac{1}{b}\] \[\frac{2}{b} = \frac{1}{a} + \frac{1}{c} = \frac{a + c}{ac}\] Inverting both sides yields the formula for the Harmonic Mean of two numbers:

\[b = \frac{2ac}{a + c}\]

The Hierarchy of Means (AM-GM-HM Inequality)

For any set of \(n\) positive real numbers, the three classical Pythagorean means obey a strict hierarchy. This is one of the most powerful and frequently tested inequalities in the entire JEE syllabus.

The AM-GM-HM Theorem

Let \(a_1, a_2, \dots, a_n\) be positive real numbers. We define their three means as:
  • Arithmetic Mean (A.M.): \(\quad A = \frac{a_1 + a_2 + \dots + a_n}{n}\)
  • Geometric Mean (G.M.): \(\quad G = (a_1 \cdot a_2 \dots a_n)^{\frac{1}{n}}\)
  • Harmonic Mean (H.M.): \(\quad H = \frac{n}{\frac{1}{a_1} + \frac{1}{a_2} + \dots + \frac{1}{a_n}}\)
These means are universally bound by the fundamental inequality:
\[A.M. \ge G.M. \ge H.M.\]
Condition for Equality: The equality (\(A = G = H\)) holds true if and only if all the individual numbers are perfectly equal to each other (\(a_1 = a_2 = \dots = a_n\)).

Relation between Means for Two Numbers

If we have exactly two positive numbers \(a\) and \(b\), their means are: \[A = \frac{a+b}{2}, \quad G = \sqrt{ab}, \quad H = \frac{2ab}{a+b}\] Notice an extraordinary algebraic property when we multiply \(A\) and \(H\): \[A \times H = \left(\frac{a+b}{2}\right) \times \left(\frac{2ab}{a+b}\right) = ab = G^2\] Thus, for two numbers, the three means actually form a Geometric Progression of their own! \[G^2 = AH\]

Applications of AM-GM in JEE

In JEE Mathematics, the AM-GM inequality is rarely tested directly as a pure sequence problem. Instead, it is an extrema-finding tool hidden within algebra, trigonometry, and calculus problems.

You should instantly consider using AM-GM whenever a problem asks you to find the:

  • Minimum value of a sum (especially when the product of the terms is constant or cancels out).
  • Maximum value of a product (especially when the sum of the terms is constant).

Example 1: Minimizing a Reciprocal Sum

Problem: Find the minimum value of \(f(x) = 4x + \frac{9}{x}\) for all \(x > 0\).
Solution: We want the minimum value of a sum of two positive terms: \(a = 4x\) and \(b = \frac{9}{x}\).

Notice that if we multiply these two terms together, the variable \(x\) completely cancels out: \[a \cdot b = (4x) \cdot \left(\frac{9}{x}\right) = 36\] Since the product is constant, the AM-GM inequality is the perfect tool! Apply AM \(\ge\) GM: \[\frac{a + b}{2} \ge \sqrt{ab}\] \[\frac{4x + \frac{9}{x}}{2} \ge \sqrt{(4x)\left(\frac{9}{x}\right)}\] \[\frac{4x + \frac{9}{x}}{2} \ge \sqrt{36}\] \[\frac{4x + \frac{9}{x}}{2} \ge 6\] Multiply by 2: \[4x + \frac{9}{x} \ge 12\] Conclusion: The absolute minimum value of the expression is 12.

(Optional Check: This minimum occurs when the two terms are equal: \(4x = \frac{9}{x} \implies x^2 = \frac{9}{4} \implies x = \frac{3}{2}\).)

Example 2: Weighted AM-GM for Asymmetric Products

Problem: If \(x, y, z\) are positive real numbers such that \(x + y + z = 12\), find the maximum value of the product \(P = x^2 y^3 z\).
Solution: We need to maximize a product \(x^2 y^3 z^1\) under a constant sum \(x + y + z = 12\).

To make the AM-GM inequality work, we must split the terms \(x, y, z\) into pieces so that their product generates the desired exponents:
  • We need \(x^2\), so we split \(x\) into 2 equal parts: \(\frac{x}{2}, \frac{x}{2}\)
  • We need \(y^3\), so we split \(y\) into 3 equal parts: \(\frac{y}{3}, \frac{y}{3}, \frac{y}{3}\)
  • We need \(z^1\), so we leave \(z\) as 1 part: \(z\)
This gives us a total of \(n = 2 + 3 + 1 = 6\) distinct terms. Let's apply AM \(\ge\) GM on these 6 positive terms: \[\frac{\frac{x}{2} + \frac{x}{2} + \frac{y}{3} + \frac{y}{3} + \frac{y}{3} + z}{6} \ge \left( \frac{x}{2} \cdot \frac{x}{2} \cdot \frac{y}{3} \cdot \frac{y}{3} \cdot \frac{y}{3} \cdot z \right)^{\frac{1}{6}}\] Notice that the numerator of the A.M. perfectly collapses back into our original sum constraint! \[\frac{x + y + z}{6} \ge \left( \frac{x^2 y^3 z}{4 \times 27} \right)^{\frac{1}{6}}\] Substitute the constraint \(x + y + z = 12\): \[\frac{12}{6} \ge \left( \frac{P}{108} \right)^{\frac{1}{6}}\] \[2 \ge \left( \frac{P}{108} \right)^{\frac{1}{6}}\] Raise both sides to the power of 6 to eliminate the radical: \[2^6 \ge \frac{P}{108}\] \[64 \ge \frac{P}{108}\] \[P \le 64 \times 108\] \[P \le 6912\] Conclusion: The maximum value of the product \(x^2 y^3 z\) is 6912. The weighted AM-GM split is an incredibly elegant trick for asymmetric maximization!
