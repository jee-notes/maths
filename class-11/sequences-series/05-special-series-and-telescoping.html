Special Series & Telescoping

Power sums, sigma notation, and the Method of Differences

Standard Power Sums (\(\Sigma\))

Before tackling complex algebraic series, it is absolutely essential to memorize the three standard power sum identities. These form the basic building blocks for evaluating polynomial sequences using Sigma notation.

The Big Three Power Sums

1. Sum of first \(n\) natural numbers:
\[\sum_{r=1}^n r = 1 + 2 + 3 + \dots + n = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}\]
2. Sum of squares of first \(n\) natural numbers:
\[\sum_{r=1}^n r^2 = 1^2 + 2^2 + 3^2 + \dots + n^2 = \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}\]
3. Sum of cubes of first \(n\) natural numbers:
\[\sum_{r=1}^n r^3 = 1^3 + 2^3 + 3^3 + \dots + n^3 = \left[ \frac{n(n+1)}{2} \right]^2\]
Observation: Notice the beautiful symmetry that the sum of the cubes is exactly the square of the sum of the numbers themselves! \(\sum r^3 = (\sum r)^2\)

Applying Sigma Notation

If the \(r^{\text{th}}\) term (\(T_r\)) of a sequence is a polynomial in \(r\), we can easily find the sum to \(n\) terms by expanding the polynomial and applying the standard power sums linearly.

For example, if \(T_r = ar^2 + br + c\), then: \[S_n = \sum_{r=1}^n T_r = a \sum_{r=1}^n r^2 + b \sum_{r=1}^n r + c \sum_{r=1}^n 1\] Note: \(\sum_{r=1}^n 1 = 1 + 1 + \dots + 1 = n\).

The Method of Differences (Telescoping Series)

When a series is not an A.P., G.P., A.G.P., or a simple polynomial sequence, we must rely on the most powerful sequence manipulation technique in mathematics: **Telescoping**.

The core philosophy of Telescoping is to artificially rewrite the \(r^{\text{th}}\) general term \(T_r\) as the difference of two consecutive states of a secondary sequence \(V_r\).

The Telescoping Mechanism

Goal: Express \(T_r = V_r - V_{r-1}\) (or sometimes \(V_{r+1} - V_r\)).

If we can achieve this split, watch what happens when we sum from \(r=1\) to \(n\): \[S_n = T_1 + T_2 + T_3 + \dots + T_n\] \[S_n = (V_1 - V_0) + (V_2 - V_1) + (V_3 - V_2) + \dots + (V_n - V_{n-1})\] Look closely at the adjacent brackets! The positive \(V_1\) cancels the negative \(V_1\). The positive \(V_2\) cancels the negative \(V_2\). This cascading cancellation collapses the entire massive sum into just the boundary terms:
\[S_n = V_n - V_0\]

Standard Telescoping Architectures

Type 1: Series of Fractions (Differences in Denominator)

Form: \(\frac{1}{a \cdot b}\) where the difference \((b - a)\) is a constant \(k\).
Trick: Multiply and divide by \(k\) to create the difference in the numerator. \[T_r = \frac{1}{k} \cdot \frac{b - a}{a \cdot b} = \frac{1}{k} \left( \frac{1}{a} - \frac{1}{b} \right)\]

Type 2: Series of Products (Consecutive Terms)

Form: \(T_r = r(r+1)(r+2)\dots(r+k-1)\) (product of \(k\) consecutive integers).
Trick: Multiply and divide by the difference of the "next" term and the "previous" term. In this case, \((r+k) - (r-1) = k+1\). \[T_r = \frac{T_r \cdot [(r+k) - (r-1)]}{k+1}\] This perfectly splits \(T_r\) into \(V_r - V_{r-1}\) where \(V_r\) has \(k+1\) factors.

Type 3: Logarithmic Telescoping

Form: \(T_r = \log \left( \frac{f(r+1)}{f(r)} \right)\)
Trick: Use the log division property directly! \[T_r = \log(f(r+1)) - \log(f(r))\] When summed, \(\sum T_r = \log(f(n+1)) - \log(f(1))\).

Solved Examples

Example 1: Classic Fraction Telescoping

Problem: Find the sum to \(n\) terms of the series: \[S_n = \frac{1}{1\cdot 2} + \frac{1}{2\cdot 3} + \frac{1}{3\cdot 4} + \dots\]
Solution: First, let's identify the general term \(T_r\). The \(r^{\text{th}}\) term has factors \(r\) and \(r+1\) in the denominator: \[T_r = \frac{1}{r(r+1)}\] Notice that the difference between the factors in the denominator is \((r+1) - r = 1\), which perfectly matches the numerator! We can split the fraction using partial fractions or logical manipulation: \[T_r = \frac{(r+1) - r}{r(r+1)} = \frac{r+1}{r(r+1)} - \frac{r}{r(r+1)}\] \[T_r = \frac{1}{r} - \frac{1}{r+1}\] This is a perfect telescoping form \(V_r - V_{r+1}\) (where \(V_r = 1/r\)). Let's write out the sum: \[S_n = \sum_{r=1}^n T_r = \sum_{r=1}^n \left( \frac{1}{r} - \frac{1}{r+1} \right)\] Expand the sum vertically to see the cascading cancellation: \[ \begin{align*} T_1 &= \left( \frac{1}{1} - \frac{1}{2} \right) \\ T_2 &= \left( \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{3} \right) \\ T_3 &= \left( \frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{4} \right) \\ &\dots \\ T_n &= \left( \frac{1}{n} - \frac{1}{n+1} \right) \end{align*} \] Adding them all together, the \(-1/2\) cancels with \(+1/2\), the \(-1/3\) cancels with \(+1/3\), and so on. Only the first positive term and the last negative term survive! \[S_n = 1 - \frac{1}{n+1}\] \[S_n = \frac{n+1-1}{n+1} = \frac{n}{n+1}\] Conclusion: The sum to \(n\) terms is \(\frac{n}{n+1}\). As \(n \to \infty\), the sum converges to exactly 1.

Example 2: Splitting Quadratic Denominators

Problem: Find the infinite sum of the series whose general term is \(T_r = \frac{2r+1}{r^2(r+1)^2}\).
Solution: At first glance, this looks incredibly complex. We need to express \(T_r\) as \(V_r - V_{r+1}\). Let's look closely at the denominator factors: \(r^2\) and \((r+1)^2\).

What is the difference between these two denominator factors? Let's calculate: \[(r+1)^2 - r^2 = (r^2 + 2r + 1) - r^2 = 2r + 1\] This difference is exactly our numerator! This is a manufactured telescoping problem. Let's rewrite the term by substituting the numerator: \[T_r = \frac{(r+1)^2 - r^2}{r^2(r+1)^2}\] Now split the fraction: \[T_r = \frac{(r+1)^2}{r^2(r+1)^2} - \frac{r^2}{r^2(r+1)^2}\] \[T_r = \frac{1}{r^2} - \frac{1}{(r+1)^2}\] We have successfully achieved telescoping form \(V_r - V_{r+1}\) with \(V_r = 1/r^2\). Let's calculate the sum to \(n\) terms (\(S_n\)): \[S_n = \sum_{r=1}^n \left( \frac{1}{r^2} - \frac{1}{(r+1)^2} \right)\] Due to the telescoping cancellation, only the very first and very last parts survive: \[S_n = \frac{1}{1^2} - \frac{1}{(n+1)^2} = 1 - \frac{1}{(n+1)^2}\] The problem asks for the infinite sum. We take the limit as \(n \to \infty\): \[S_\infty = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left( 1 - \frac{1}{(n+1)^2} \right)\] Since the denominator \((n+1)^2\) grows infinitely large, the fraction approaches zero. \[S_\infty = 1 - 0 = 1\] Conclusion: The infinite sum evaluates perfectly to 1.
