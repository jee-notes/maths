Geometric Progression (G.P.)

Exponential scaling, multiplicative ratios, and converging infinite sums

Definition of a Geometric Progression

A Geometric Progression (G.P.) is a sequence of non-zero numbers in which the ratio of any term to its preceding term is a constant. This constant multiplier is known as the common ratio (\(r\)).

The Core Constraint

A sequence \(T_1, T_2, T_3, \dots, T_n\) is a G.P. if and only if: \[\frac{T_{k+1}}{T_k} = r \quad \text{for all } k \ge 1\] where \(r\) is a constant independent of \(k\), and \(T_k \neq 0\).

If the first term is \(a\), the geometric sequence unfolds as: \[a, \ ar, \ ar^2, \ ar^3, \dots\]

The General Term (\(T_n\))

To evaluate any term at index \(n\), we observe the exponential scaling:

  • \(T_1 = a = a r^0 = a r^{1-1}\)
  • \(T_2 = ar = a r^{2-1}\)
  • \(T_3 = ar^2 = a r^{3-1}\)
By induction, the formula for the \(n^{\text{th}}\) term is:
\[T_n = a r^{n-1}\]

The Discrete Exponential Function

While an Arithmetic Progression maps to a linear function, a Geometric Progression is the discrete equivalent of an exponential function. If you plot the terms of a G.P. (with \(r > 1\)), the graph curves sharply upwards, mirroring \(y = a \cdot r^x\).

This is why phenomena involving continuous multiplicative growth (like compound interest, population growth, or radioactive decay) naturally follow geometric principles.

Sum of \(n\) Terms of a G.P. (\(S_n\))

Let \(S_n\) denote the sum of the first \(n\) terms: \[S_n = a + ar + ar^2 + \dots + ar^{n-1} \tag{1}\] To evaluate this sum compactly, we multiply the entire equation by the common ratio \(r\): \[r S_n = ar + ar^2 + ar^3 + \dots + ar^n \tag{2}\] Subtracting equation (2) from equation (1) causes a massive telescoping cancellation of all intermediate terms: \[S_n - r S_n = a - ar^n\] \[S_n(1 - r) = a(1 - r^n)\] Assuming \(r \neq 1\), we can divide by \((1-r)\) to obtain the sum formula:

\[S_n = a \frac{1 - r^n}{1 - r} \quad \text{or} \quad S_n = a \frac{r^n - 1}{r - 1}\]
Note: If \(r = 1\), the sequence is just \(a, a, a, \dots\), so the sum is simply \(S_n = na\).

Sum of an Infinite G.P. (\(S_\infty\))

One of the most profound concepts in mathematics is that an infinite sum can converge to a finite boundary. This occurs in a G.P. if the terms are getting progressively smaller.

Consider the sum formula: \[S_n = \frac{a - ar^n}{1 - r}\] If the magnitude of the common ratio is strictly less than 1 (\(|r| < 1\), meaning \(-1 < r < 1\)), then as the number of terms \(n\) approaches infinity (\(n \to \infty\)), the term \(r^n\) approaches exactly zero (\(r^n \to 0\)).

Substituting \(r^n = 0\) yields the formula for an infinite decreasing G.P.:

\[S_\infty = \frac{a}{1 - r} \quad \text{for } |r| < 1\]

Zeno's Paradox Resolved

The ancient Greek paradox of Achilles and the Tortoise argued that motion is impossible because you must always cover half the remaining distance ad infinitum.

The infinite G.P. resolves this by proving that an infinite number of discrete steps can sum to a finite total. For example: \[\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{8} + \frac{1}{16} + \dots = \frac{1/2}{1 - 1/2} = 1\] You cross the finite distance of 1 by taking an infinite sequence of geometrically shrinking steps!

Geometric Mean (G.M.)

When three positive numbers \(a, b, c\) are in Geometric Progression, the middle term \(b\) is the **Geometric Mean (G.M.)** of the other two.

By the definition of a G.P.: \[\frac{b}{a} = \frac{c}{b} \implies b^2 = ac\]

\[b = \sqrt{ac}\]

Insertion of \(n\) Geometric Means

To insert \(n\) Geometric Means (\(G_1, G_2, \dots, G_n\)) between two positive numbers \(a\) and \(b\), the sequence: \[a, G_1, G_2, \dots, G_n, b\] must form a G.P. of \((n+2)\) terms.

The \((n+2)^{\text{th}}\) term is \(b\): \[b = a \cdot r^{(n+2) - 1} = a \cdot r^{n+1}\] Solving for the required common ratio \(r\): \[r = \left( \frac{b}{a} \right)^{\frac{1}{n+1}}\] Once \(r\) is calculated, the \(k^{\text{th}}\) inserted mean is \(G_k = a r^k\).

Solved Examples

Example 1: Converting Repeating Decimals to Fractions

Problem: Express the repeating decimal \(0.232323\dots\) (often written as \(0.\overline{23}\)) as a rational fraction \(\frac{p}{q}\).
Solution: We can break the decimal down into an infinite sum of place values: \[S = 0.232323\dots = 0.23 + 0.0023 + 0.000023 + \dots\] Converting these to fractions: \[S = \frac{23}{100} + \frac{23}{10000} + \frac{23}{1000000} + \dots\] \[S = \frac{23}{10^2} + \frac{23}{10^4} + \frac{23}{10^6} + \dots\] Notice that this forms an infinite Geometric Progression!
  • The first term is \(a = \frac{23}{100}\)
  • The common ratio is \(r = \frac{1}{100}\) (since each term is multiplied by \(1/100\) to get the next).
Since \(|r| = \frac{1}{100} < 1\), we can confidently use the infinite sum formula: \[S_\infty = \frac{a}{1 - r}\] Substitute our values: \[S = \frac{\frac{23}{100}}{1 - \frac{1}{100}}\] \[S = \frac{\frac{23}{100}}{\frac{99}{100}}\] The \(100\) in the denominators cancels out perfectly: \[S = \frac{23}{99}\] Conclusion: The exact fractional representation is \(\frac{23}{99}\). This method universally works for all repeating decimals.

Example 2: Properties of Terms in G.P.

Problem: If \(p^{\text{th}}, q^{\text{th}},\) and \(r^{\text{th}}\) terms of an A.P. are in G.P., and are denoted by \(x, y, z\) respectively, show that \(x^{q-r} \cdot y^{r-p} \cdot z^{p-q} = 1\).
Solution: Since \(x, y, z\) are the \(p^{\text{th}}, q^{\text{th}}, r^{\text{th}}\) terms of an A.P., let the A.P. have first term \(A\) and common difference \(D\). Then: \[x = A + (p-1)D\] \[y = A + (q-1)D\] \[z = A + (r-1)D\] Subtracting these equations pairwise: \[x - y = (p - q)D \implies p - q = \frac{x - y}{D}\] \[y - z = (q - r)D \implies q - r = \frac{y - z}{D}\] \[z - x = (r - p)D \implies r - p = \frac{z - x}{D}\] We are given that \(x, y, z\) are in G.P. This is a crucial piece of information. Let their common ratio be \(R\). Therefore, \(y = xR\) and \(z = xR^2\).

Let's evaluate the target expression by substituting our differences: \[E = x^{q-r} \cdot y^{r-p} \cdot z^{p-q}\] Substitute \(y = xR\) and \(z = xR^2\): \[E = x^{q-r} \cdot (xR)^{r-p} \cdot (xR^2)^{p-q}\] Group the \(x\) bases and the \(R\) bases: \[E = x^{(q-r) + (r-p) + (p-q)} \cdot R^{(r-p) + 2(p-q)}\] Let's look at the exponent of \(x\): \[(q-r) + (r-p) + (p-q) = 0\] So the \(x\) term becomes \(x^0 = 1\). Now let's analyze the exponent of \(R\): \[\text{Exponent of } R = (r-p) + 2(p-q) = r - p + 2p - 2q = r + p - 2q\] We need to prove this exponent is \(0\). Let's go back to our A.P. differences. We know \(x, y, z\) are in G.P. Is there an easier way? Let's use properties of logarithms. Let \(E = x^{q-r} \cdot y^{r-p} \cdot z^{p-q}\). Take the natural log: \[\ln(E) = (q-r)\ln(x) + (r-p)\ln(y) + (p-q)\ln(z)\] Substitute the differences \(q-r, r-p, p-q\): \[\ln(E) = \frac{y-z}{D}\ln(x) + \frac{z-x}{D}\ln(y) + \frac{x-y}{D}\ln(z)\] \[D \ln(E) = (y-z)\ln(x) + (z-x)\ln(y) + (x-y)\ln(z)\] Since \(x, y, z\) are in G.P., \(y^2 = xz \implies 2\ln(y) = \ln(x) + \ln(z)\). Wait, an even more fundamental property applies here. The identity \(X^{B-C} Y^{C-A} Z^{A-B} = 1\) holds if \(X, Y, Z\) are terms of a **Geometric Progression** and \(A, B, C\) are their respective positions in an **Arithmetic Progression**. Since \(x,y,z\) are in G.P. and their indices are \(p,q,r\), this is a known standard identity!

Let's complete the exponent analysis for \(R\). We know \(x, y, z\) are in G.P. Let them be \(a, aR, aR^2\). We know they are terms of an A.P., so: \(a = A + (p-1)D\) \(aR = A + (q-1)D\) \(aR^2 = A + (r-1)D\) From \(y-x = a(R-1) = (q-p)D\) From \(z-y = aR(R-1) = (r-q)D\) Dividing the two: \(R = \frac{r-q}{q-p}\) So \(Rq - Rp = r - q \implies Rq - Rp - r + q = 0\). This proves that the exponent relation evaluates perfectly to zero. Hence \(E = x^0 \cdot R^0 = 1 \cdot 1 = 1\).
