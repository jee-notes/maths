Inequalities & Wavy Curve Method

Understanding algebraic ordering, rational inequalities, and the sign scheme algorithm

Introduction to Inequalities

Unlike equations which identify specific points of equality, inequalities describe continuous regions on the real number line where one expression is greater than or less than another.

The four fundamental inequality symbols are:

  • \(a > b\) : \(a\) is strictly greater than \(b\).
  • \(a < b\) : \(a\) is strictly less than \(b\).
  • \(a \ge b\) : \(a\) is greater than or equal to \(b\).
  • \(a \le b\) : \(a\) is less than or equal to \(b\).

Fundamental Properties of Inequalities (Let \(a, b, c \in \mathbb{R}\))

  1. Addition/Subtraction Property: You can add or subtract any number on both sides without changing the inequality sign: \[a > b \implies a + c > b + c \quad \text{and} \quad a - c > b - c\]
  2. Positive Multiplication/Division: Multiplying or dividing by a positive number preserves the inequality: \[a > b \implies ac > bc \quad (\text{if } c > 0)\]
  3. Negative Multiplication/Division (CRITICAL): Multiplying or dividing by a negative number reverses the inequality: \[a > b \implies ac < bc \quad (\text{if } c < 0)\]
  4. Reciprocals:
    • If \(a\) and \(b\) have the same sign: \(a > b \implies \frac{1}{a} < \frac{1}{b}\)
    • If \(a\) and \(b\) have different signs: \(a > b \implies \frac{1}{a} > \frac{1}{b}\) (positive is always greater than negative!)

The Danger of Cross-Multiplication

In equations, we cross-multiply terms across the equals sign routinely. In inequalities, **never cross-multiply by an expression containing a variable** (like \(x-2\)) unless you are 100% certain of its sign.

If \(x-2\) is negative, cross-multiplying will secretly reverse the inequality. If it is positive, it won't. Since \(x\) varies, cross-multiplying without casing will lead to completely incorrect solutions. Instead, always subtract to make one side zero and solve using a unified fraction!

Polynomial and Rational Inequalities

A rational inequality is any inequality that can be written in the form: \[\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)} \ge 0 \quad \text{or} \quad \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)} < 0\] where \(P(x)\) and \(Q(x)\) are polynomials.

To solve these expressions, we must analyze the collective sign of the factors. The primary rule is that the expression can only change its sign when passing through values of \(x\) where either the numerator is zero (\(P(x) = 0\)) or the denominator is zero (\(Q(x) = 0\)). These transition boundary points are called **critical points**.

Denominator Exclusion Rule

Regardless of whether the inequality is strict (\(<, >\)) or non-strict (\(\le, \ge\)), the denominator \(Q(x)\) can **never be zero**: \[Q(x) \neq 0\] Therefore, the roots of the denominator must **always** be represented with open parentheses in the final interval notation. Including a denominator root is one of the most common errors in JEE!

The Wavy Curve Method (Sign Scheme)

The Wavy Curve Method is a highly systematic algorithm used to solve polynomial and rational inequalities. It uses the multiplicity of roots to determine the signs of intervals across the real number line.

The Step-by-Step Algorithm

  1. Make One Side Zero: Transpose all terms to the left-hand side so that the right-hand side is \(0\). Combine the LHS into a single rational fraction: \(\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}\).
  2. Linear Factorization: Factorize both \(P(x)\) and \(Q(x)\) completely into linear factors of the form \((x - \alpha)^k\).
  3. Normalize leading coefficients: Ensure that the coefficient of the variable \(x\) in every linear factor is **positive** (ideally \(+1\)). If a factor is \((2-x)\), write it as \(-(x-2)\). Multiply the entire inequality by \(-1\) if needed, and **reverse the inequality direction**.
  4. Identify Critical Points: Find all roots of the numerator and denominator by setting each linear factor equal to zero.
  5. Plot on Number Line: Plot these critical points on a number line in ascending order.
  6. Apply the Wave Rules (Right-to-Left):
    • The rightmost interval (greater than the largest critical point) is **always positive** (\(+\)).
    • Moving from right to left across a critical point \(\alpha\):
      • If the critical point corresponds to a factor with an **odd exponent** (e.g., \((x-\alpha)^1\), \((x-\alpha)^3\)), the sign **changes** (\(+\) to \(-\), or \(-\) to \(+\)).
      • If the critical point corresponds to a factor with an **even exponent** (e.g., \((x-\alpha)^2\), \((x-\alpha)^4\)), the sign **remains unchanged** (\(+\) to \(+\), or \(-\) to \(-\)).
  7. Write the Solution Set: Write down the interval representation matching the target sign (positive intervals for \(>, \ge\); negative intervals for \(<, \le\)). Make sure to apply the **Denominator Exclusion Rule**.

Comprehensive Solved Example

Wavy Curve Method in Action

Problem: Solve for real \(x\): \[\frac{(x-1)^3(x+2)^2}{(x-3)(x+4)} \ge 0\]
Solution:
  1. Check Normalization: The right side is \(0\), and the LHS is a single rational fraction. In each factor (\((x-1)\), \((x+2)\), \((x-3)\), \((x+4)\)), the coefficient of \(x\) is \(+1\) (already normalized).
  2. Identify Critical Points and Multiplicities:
    • Numerator Roots:
      • \(x = 1\) (from factor \((x-1)^3\), odd exponent \(3\) \(\rightarrow\) sign changes)
      • \(x = -2\) (from factor \((x+2)^2\), even exponent \(2\) \(\rightarrow\) sign remains same)
    • Denominator Roots (Must be excluded from solution):
      • \(x = 3\) (from factor \((x-3)^1\), odd exponent \(1\) \(\rightarrow\) sign changes)
      • \(x = -4\) (from factor \((x+4)^1\), odd exponent \(1\) \(\rightarrow\) sign changes)
  3. Plot and Assign Signs (Right-to-Left): Plot critical points on the number line: \(-4\), \(-2\), \(1\), \(3\).
    • Interval \((3, \infty)\): Rightmost interval is **positive (\(+\))**.
    • Passing through \(x = 3\) (odd exponent): Sign changes to **negative (\(-\))**. Interval \((1, 3)\) is \(-\).
    • Passing through \(x = 1\) (odd exponent): Sign changes to **positive (\(+\))**. Interval \((-2, 1)\) is \(+\).
    • Passing through \(x = -2\) (even exponent): Sign remains the same, i.e., **positive (\(+\))**. Interval \((-4, -2)\) is \(+\point\).
    • Passing through \(x = -4\) (odd exponent): Sign changes to **negative (\(-\))**. Interval \((-\infty, -4)\) is \(-\).
  4. Map the Sign Wave:
    Interval Sign of Expression Status in Solution (\(\ge 0\))
    \((3, \infty)\) \(+\) Included (\(3\) is excluded due to denominator root)
    \((1, 3)\) \(-\) Excluded
    \((-2, 1]\) \(+\) Included (\(1\) is included since it makes numerator \(0\))
    \((-4, -2)\) \(+\) Included (\(-2\) is included since it makes numerator \(0\), joining the intervals)
    \((-\infty, -4)\) \(-\) Excluded (\(-4\) is excluded due to denominator root)
  5. Formulate Final Interval: We need the union of positive intervals: \[x \in (-4, -2] \cup [-2, 1] \cup (3, \infty)\] Notice that \(x = -2\) is included in both intervals, which makes them contiguous. Thus, they merge into a single interval: \[x \in (-4, 1] \cup (3, \infty)\]
Final Answer: \[x \in (-4, 1] \cup (3, \infty)\]

Absolute Value (Modulus) Inequalities

The absolute value \(|x|\) represents the geometric distance of a number \(x\) from the origin \(0\) on the number line. Distance is inherently non-negative.

For any positive real number \(a > 0\), the standard modulus inequalities can be rewritten as bounded intervals:

Core Modulus Properties

  1. \(|x| \le a \iff -a \le x \le a \quad (x \in [-a, a])\)
  2. \(|x| \ge a \iff x \le -a \quad \text{or} \quad x \ge a \quad (x \in (-\infty, -a] \cup [a, \infty))\)
  3. \(|x| < a \iff -a < x < a \quad (x \in (-a, a))\)
  4. \(|x| > a \iff x < -a \quad \text{or} \quad x > a \quad (x \in (-\infty, -a) \cup (a, \infty))\)

Solved Example: Solving a Modulus Inequality

Problem: Solve for \(x\): \[|2x - 5| \le 9\]
Solution: Using Property 1, we can remove the modulus signs by placing the expression inside a three-part inequality: \[-9 \le 2x - 5 \le 9\] Add \(5\) to all three parts to isolate the term with \(x\): \[-9 + 5 \le 2x \le 9 + 5\] \[-4 \le 2x \le 14\] Divide all parts by the positive number \(2\) (preserving the inequality signs): \[-2 \le x \le 7\] In interval notation: \[x \in [-2, 7]\]
